## VII. Third-Party Claims

### NRS 616C.215 — Third-Party Liability
- When a third party (not employer/co-worker) causes work injury, employee may pursue both WC benefits and third-party tort claim
- Examples: vehicle accident caused by non-employee driver, defective equipment manufacturer, negligent property owner
- WC insurer has subrogation rights against third-party recovery

### SB 258 Lien Caps
- Limits insurer's recovery from third-party settlements
- Insurer cannot recover more than it has paid in benefits
- Employee's attorney fees and costs reduce the lien proportionally
- Common fund doctrine applies — insurer benefits from employee's legal efforts

### Subrogation Process
1. Insurer has right to be notified of any third-party claim
2. Insurer may intervene in third-party litigation
3. Settlement requires insurer consent or court approval
4. Lien amount calculated: total benefits paid minus proportional share of attorney fees/costs
5. Net recovery to employee after lien satisfaction

### Strategy Considerations
- Pursue third-party claims whenever viable — additional recovery beyond WC benefits
- Coordinate timing: WC benefits provide ongoing support during third-party litigation
- Negotiate lien reduction aggressively using SB 258 and common fund arguments
- Consider employer negligence (rare exception to exclusive remedy)
- Document all third-party liability evidence early

### Exclusive Remedy Doctrine
- WC is generally exclusive remedy against employer for workplace injuries
- Exceptions:
  - Employer's willful and deliberate conduct (NRS 616A.020)
  - Dual capacity doctrine (limited application)
  - Third-party claims against non-employer entities
- Co-employee immunity: generally immune unless intentional act
