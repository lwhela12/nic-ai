## XIII. Elder Tax Planning

### Medical Expense Deduction (IRC §213)
- Deduct unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding **7.5% of AGI**
- **Deductible elder care costs**:
  - Nursing home (if primarily for medical care — **100% deductible**)
  - Assisted living (medical care portion; facility must document)
  - In-home care (if medically necessary — portion related to medical care)
  - LTC insurance premiums (age-based limits: age 61–70 = $5,640; age 71+ = **$7,050** for 2025)
  - Medications, medical equipment, doctor visits, dental, vision
  - Home modifications for medical necessity (ramps, grab bars, widened doorways)
  - Transportation to medical appointments (67 cents/mile for 2025)
- **Bunching strategy**: time elective medical expenses into one year to exceed 7.5% threshold

### Home Sale Exclusion (IRC §121)
- Exclude up to **$250,000** (single) / **$500,000** (married) capital gain on primary residence sale
- Must have owned and used as primary residence **2 of 5 years** before sale
- **Nursing home exception**: if taxpayer lived in facility due to physical/mental incapacity, only need **1 year** of the 5 in the home (RCW N/A — federal rule)
- For married couples: both spouses must meet ownership test; one must meet use test
- **Caution**: selling home may affect Medicaid eligibility if proceeds not properly managed

### Credit for the Elderly or Disabled (IRC §22)
- Available to taxpayers **age 65+** or permanently disabled
- Small credit: maximum **$1,125** (married filing jointly, both 65+)
- Phased out at modest income levels (AGI > $25,000 single / $32,500 joint)
- Rarely beneficial for those with Social Security income but worth checking for very low-income elders

### Trust Taxation
- **Grantor trusts** (revocable living trusts): income taxed on grantor's individual return — no separate trust return during lifetime
- **Irrevocable trusts**: file Form 1041; trust tax brackets are **compressed** — reach highest rate (37%) at just **$15,200** of income (2025)
- Strategy: **distribute trust income** to beneficiaries in lower tax brackets when possible
- Trust can deduct distributions to beneficiaries (distributable net income)
- **Electing Small Business Trust (ESBT)** and **Qualified Subchapter S Trust (QSST)**: special rules for trusts holding S-corp stock

### Washington State Tax Considerations
- **No state income tax** in Washington
- **7% capital gains tax** on gains > $270,000 (2025 threshold, adjusted annually) — long-term gains on stocks/bonds; excludes real estate and retirement accounts
- **WA estate tax**: see Section IV for rates and thresholds
- **Property tax**: seniors 61+ with income ≤ $84,000 may qualify for **property tax exemption or deferral** (RCW 84.36.381)
  - Exemption: partial to full based on income
  - Deferral: postpone taxes until sale or death (interest accrues)
- **Sales tax**: no exemption for seniors but prescription medications are exempt

### Roth Conversion Strategy
- Convert Traditional IRA to Roth IRA — pay income tax now, tax-free growth and withdrawals later
- **Best years to convert**: early retirement before Social Security/RMDs start (lower income years)
- Avoid converting so much that it pushes into higher IRMAA bracket (affects Medicare premiums 2 years later)
- Roth conversions can reduce future RMDs, reduce Social Security taxation, and provide tax-free inheritance
- **No income limit** for conversions (unlike Roth IRA contributions)
- Conversion amount is added to gross income for the year — plan carefully around tax brackets

### Tax Filing Tips for Elders
- **Standard deduction**: additional **$1,950** (single 65+) or **$1,550** per spouse (married 65+) for 2025
- Social Security worksheet: determine taxable portion (0%, 50%, or 85%)
- File even if not required — may qualify for refundable credits
- **IRS Free File**: available for AGI ≤ $84,000; AARP Tax-Aide provides free preparation
- Consider **Form 56** (Notice of Fiduciary Relationship) if POA agent is filing on behalf of incapacitated person
