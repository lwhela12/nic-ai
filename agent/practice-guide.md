# PI Practice Guide

Comprehensive practice knowledge for Personal Injury case evaluation, valuation, and management. This document is injected into agent prompts — edit sections here to update agent behavior firm-wide.

---

## I. Liability Evaluation

### Clear Liability (90-100% favorable)
- Rear-end collisions (presumption of negligence on following driver)
- Left-turn accidents where adverse ran red light or failed to yield
- DUI/impaired driver at fault
- Police report assigns fault to adverse party
- Citation issued to adverse driver
- Multiple independent witnesses corroborate client's account

### Moderate Liability (60-89% favorable)
- Disputed right-of-way with supporting evidence (dash cam, witness)
- Lane-change accidents with partial corroboration
- Parking lot accidents with unclear right-of-way
- Police report is neutral or no report filed
- Minor comparative fault arguments possible but weak

### Contested Liability (below 60%)
- Conflicting witness accounts
- Client received citation
- Multi-vehicle accidents with unclear chain of causation
- Possible comparative fault exceeding 20%
- No independent witnesses, word vs. word

### Comparative Fault Framework (Nevada NRS 41.141)
- Modified comparative fault: plaintiff recovers if **less than 50% at fault**
- Recovery reduced by plaintiff's percentage of fault
- 50% or more = total bar to recovery
- Assess comparative fault exposure early; document mitigating factors
- Common comparative fault arguments: distracted driving, failure to mitigate, seatbelt defense (limited in NV)

## II. Injury Severity Assessment

### Tier 1: Soft Tissue (Sprains/Strains)
- Cervical/lumbar strain without objective imaging findings
- Treatment: chiropractic, physical therapy, massage
- Duration typically 8-16 weeks
- No injections or surgical referrals
- Key weakness: defense labels as "MIST" (Minor Impact Soft Tissue)

### Tier 2: Moderate Structural
- Disc bulge or protrusion on MRI
- Radiculopathy with objective findings (EMG/NCV positive)
- Treatment includes injections (epidural, facet, trigger point)
- Duration 4-12 months
- Potential for future care needs

### Tier 3: Severe / Surgical
- Disc herniation requiring surgery (discectomy, fusion, arthroplasty)
- Fractures requiring surgical repair
- TBI with documented cognitive deficits
- Shoulder/knee tears requiring arthroscopy or reconstruction
- Permanent impairment ratings assigned

### Objective Findings That Strengthen Cases
- MRI showing disc pathology correlating with symptoms
- Positive EMG/NCV studies
- Surgical intervention required
- Documented loss of range of motion (measured, not subjective)
- Impairment rating from treating physician
- Consistent complaints across multiple providers

### Red Flags That Weaken Injury Claims
- No objective imaging findings despite prolonged treatment
- Symptom magnification noted by providers
- Treatment gaps exceeding 2 weeks in first 3 months
- Prior accidents or pre-existing conditions at same spinal level
- Minimal property damage with claimed severe injuries
- IME opinion contradicting treating physician

## III. Treatment Pattern Analysis

### Expected Treatment Progressions
**Soft Tissue (Tier 1):**
1. ER or urgent care within 72 hours of accident
2. Chiropractic/PT begins within 1-2 weeks
3. Treatment 2-3x/week for 6-12 weeks, tapering
4. MMI reached by 3-4 months
5. Total visits: 24-48

**Moderate/Structural (Tier 2):**
1. ER or urgent care → PCP referral within 1 week
2. Conservative care (chiro/PT) 4-8 weeks
3. MRI ordered when conservative care plateaus
4. Pain management referral for injections
5. 2-3 injection series over 3-6 months
6. MMI or surgical consult at 6-12 months

**Surgical (Tier 3):**
1. ER → orthopedic/neurosurgical consult
2. Conservative care trial (may be abbreviated)
3. Advanced imaging confirming surgical pathology
4. Surgical intervention
5. Post-surgical rehabilitation 3-6 months
6. MMI with permanent restrictions/impairment

### Treatment Red Flags
- **Gaps > 14 days** in first 3 months — defense argues injuries resolved
- **Excessive treatment duration** without escalation or objective worsening
- **No imaging ordered** despite 3+ months of treatment
- **Treatment only at lien providers** — defense argues financial motivation
- **Attorney-referred treatment** starting weeks/months post-accident
- **Inconsistent attendance** — missed appointments undermine severity claims

### Gap Significance
- 1-7 days: generally not an issue, can explain as weekend/scheduling
- 8-14 days: document reason (illness, work, travel)
- 15-30 days: significant — defense will argue symptoms resolved
- 30+ days: severe damage to case — requires compelling explanation (hospitalization, family emergency with documentation)

## IV. Valuation Framework

### Multiplier Guidelines by Injury Tier

| Tier | Injury Type | Multiplier Range | Notes |
|------|------------|-----------------|-------|
| 1 | Soft tissue, no imaging findings | 1.5-2.5x specials | Lower end if MIST defense likely |
| 1+ | Soft tissue with injections | 2-3x specials | Injections show severity beyond basic strain |
| 2 | Disc/structural on imaging | 2.5-4x specials | Higher if radiculopathy confirmed |
| 3 | Surgery performed | 3-5x+ specials | Higher for fusion, multi-level, complications |

### Policy Limits Demand Triggers
- Specials exceed **40% of 3P per-person BI limit** (soft tissue)
- Specials exceed **30% of 3P per-person BI limit** (structural/disc injury)
- Any surgical case — almost always demand policy limits
- Calculated demand (specials x multiplier) exceeds 3P limit

### Value-Increasing Factors
- Clear liability (rear-end, DUI, citation to adverse)
- Objective findings (MRI+, EMG+, surgical pathology)
- Surgery performed or recommended
- Documented work impact (missed days, reduced capacity, job loss)
- Consistent treatment without gaps
- Sympathetic client (elderly, child, pregnant)
- Good property damage photos showing severity
- Pre-accident active lifestyle documented

### Value-Decreasing Factors
- Pre-existing conditions at same spinal level
- Treatment gaps exceeding 14 days
- Comparative fault exposure
- Low property damage (MIST defense)
- Prior PI claims or lawsuits
- Social media contradicting injury claims
- Delayed treatment onset (>7 days post-accident)
- Minimal lost wages despite claimed severity

## V. Negotiation Strategy

### Demand Positioning
- **Policy limits demand:** Use when specials justify it (see Section IV triggers). Include 30-day time-limit language. Reference bad faith exposure.
- **Specific amount demand:** Set initial demand at top of reasonable range. Leave room for negotiation — first offer should be 80-100% of target recovery.
- **Never demand more than 3P policy limits.** The insurer cannot pay more than the policy allows.

### Counter-Offer Response Tactics
1. **Lowball first offer (< 50% of specials):** Respond with detailed rebuttal letter. Do not counter yet — force adjuster to justify their number.
2. **Reasonable first offer (50-80% of demand):** Counter at 10-15% below your demand. Signal willingness to close.
3. **Near-limits offer:** Accept promptly or counter minimally. Delays risk policy cancellation or bankruptcy.

### Bad Faith Leverage (Nevada)
- Nevada recognizes first-party and third-party bad faith claims
- Insurer must conduct reasonable investigation before denying/lowballing
- Failure to respond to time-limit demand within deadline creates bad faith exposure
- Document all unreasonable delays, lowball offers, and failure to investigate
- Bad faith damages can include emotional distress and punitive damages

### Settlement Timing Considerations
- Do not settle until client reaches MMI (Maximum Medical Improvement)
- Exception: SOL pressure — settle if SOL is imminent and litigation not viable
- Consider future medical needs before accepting — releases are final
- Med-pay should be exhausted before settling 3P claim

## VI. Defense Anticipation & Red Flags

### Common Insurer Tactics
1. **MIST defense** (Minor Impact Soft Tissue): Low property damage = minor injuries. Counter with biomechanical research, treating physician testimony.
2. **Pre-existing condition:** "Degenerative changes" on MRI used to deny causation. Counter with aggravation/exacerbation doctrine — tortfeasor takes victim as found (eggshell plaintiff).
3. **IME opinions:** Defense medical exams often minimize injuries. Prepare client, obtain records of IME doctor's testimony history.
4. **Surveillance:** Insurers may surveil clients. Advise clients: be honest about limitations, avoid social media activity contradicting claims.
5. **Recorded statements:** Decline or prepare client thoroughly. Early recorded statements often used to lock in minimized symptoms.
6. **Delay tactics:** Adjuster non-responsiveness, repeated document requests. Counter with time-limit demands and bad faith documentation.

### Red Flags in Our Own Cases
- Client non-compliance with treatment
- Client active on social media showing physical activity
- Inconsistent symptom reporting across providers
- Client has multiple prior PI claims
- Client changed attorneys during case
- Provider billing significantly above usual/customary rates

## VII. UIM/UM Claims

### Trigger Conditions
- **UM (Uninsured Motorist):** Adverse party has no insurance or is hit-and-run
- **UIM (Underinsured Motorist):** Damages exceed adverse party's 3P limits
- UIM triggered only AFTER 3P limits exhausted or settlement reached

### Procedural Requirements (Nevada)
1. Exhaust 3P claim first (settle or confirm limits inadequate)
2. Notify 1P carrier of potential UIM claim early
3. Get 1P carrier consent before settling 3P claim (policy requirement)
4. File UIM demand with 1P carrier
5. Arbitration clause common — check policy language

### Stacking Rules (Nevada NRS 687B.145)
- Nevada allows stacking of UM/UIM coverage across multiple vehicles on same policy
- Inter-policy stacking (multiple policies) may apply — check all household policies
- Stacking can significantly increase available coverage

### UIM Demand Strategy
- Calculate total damages minus 3P recovery = UIM demand amount
- Cannot recover more than UIM policy limits
- UIM carrier entitled to credit for 3P settlement amount
- Consider whether UIM demand justifies litigation/arbitration costs

## VIII. Subrogation & Lien Handling

### Lien Types and Reduction Expectations

| Lien Type | Typical Reduction | Notes |
|-----------|------------------|-------|
| Private health insurance (non-ERISA) | 40-60% | Negotiate using made-whole doctrine, common fund |
| ERISA self-funded plans | 0-20% | Federal preemption limits reduction ability; check plan language |
| Medicare | Per formula | Medicare Secondary Payer Act; use procurement costs argument |
| Medicaid | 30-50% | State-specific; Nevada allows equitable reduction |
| Hospital liens (NRS 108.590) | 20-40% | Statutory lien; negotiate based on case value |
| Medical provider liens (letter of protection) | 30-50% | Negotiate based on case economics; get agreements in writing |
| Med-Pay subrogation | Often waived | Fee typically not charged on Med-Pay recovery |

### ERISA vs Non-ERISA Determination
- **ERISA (self-funded):** Employer pays claims directly; plan document says "self-funded" or "self-insured." Federal law governs — harder to reduce.
- **Non-ERISA (fully insured):** Insurance company bears risk. State law applies — easier to reduce using made-whole and common fund doctrines.
- **How to check:** Request plan document (SPD). Look for "funded by" or "claims paid by" language.

### Reduction Negotiation Process
1. Identify all liens before settlement
2. Request itemized lien amounts with supporting documentation
3. Calculate proportional reduction based on attorney fees and costs
4. Present common fund argument (lienholder benefits from attorney's work)
5. Get reduction agreements **in writing** before disbursement
6. Never disburse settlement funds until all liens resolved

## IX. Case Management Workflow

### Phase Objectives

| Phase | Primary Objective | Key Actions |
|-------|------------------|-------------|
| Intake | Establish representation | Send LORs, gather client info, open claims |
| Investigation | Build liability case | Obtain police report, photos, witness statements, dec pages |
| Treatment | Document injuries fully | Monitor treatment, order records/bills, track gaps |
| Demand | Present case for settlement | Draft demand, compile exhibits, send with deadline |
| Negotiation | Maximize recovery | Respond to counters, evaluate litigation vs. settlement |
| Settlement | Resolve and disburse | Negotiate liens, prepare disbursement, get releases signed |

### Timeline Management
- **Day 1-7:** Send LORs, open claims, schedule client for treatment
- **Week 2-4:** Follow up on dec pages, order police report
- **Monthly:** Check treatment compliance, request updated records
- **At MMI:** Order final records/bills, balance confirmations
- **Post-MMI (2-4 weeks):** Draft and send demand
- **Demand response (30-45 days):** Negotiate or evaluate litigation

### SOL Pressure Points
- **12 months before SOL:** Begin demand preparation regardless of treatment status
- **6 months before SOL:** Demand must be sent; evaluate litigation filing
- **90 days before SOL:** If no settlement, file complaint to preserve claim
- **SOL date:** Nevada PI = 2 years from DOL (NRS 11.190(4)(e))

## X. Nevada-Specific Law

### Statute of Limitations
- **Personal Injury:** 2 years from date of injury (NRS 11.190(4)(e))
- **Property Damage:** 3 years (NRS 11.190(3)(c))
- **Medical Malpractice:** 3 years from injury or 1 year from discovery (NRS 41A.097)
- **Wrongful Death:** 2 years (NRS 11.190(4)(e))
- **Minors:** SOL tolled until age 18, then standard period runs

### Comparative Negligence (NRS 41.141)
- Modified comparative fault: plaintiff recovers if **less than 50% at fault**
- Recovery reduced by plaintiff's percentage of fault
- 50% or more at fault = complete bar to recovery
- Jury determines comparative fault percentages

### Insurance Minimums (NRS 485.185)
- $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident bodily injury
- $20,000 property damage
- UM/UIM coverage required at same minimums unless rejected in writing

### Med-Pay
- First-party coverage, no-fault — pays regardless of who caused accident
- Typically $1,000 - $10,000
- Often subrogated; fee usually waived on Med-Pay recovery
- Exhaust Med-Pay before settling 3P claim when possible

### Venue Considerations
- Clark County (Las Vegas): generally plaintiff-friendly for PI cases
- Washoe County (Reno): moderate
- Rural counties: more conservative, lower verdicts
- Federal court: only if diversity jurisdiction and amount in controversy > $75,000

### Collateral Source Rule (NRS 42.021)
- Evidence of collateral source payments (health insurance) generally inadmissible
- Prevents defense from arguing plaintiff was "made whole" by insurance
- Exception: subrogation/lien rights preserved

## XI. Document Quality Assessment

### Completeness Checklist
**Required for every case:**
- [ ] Signed retainer agreement
- [ ] Client intake form with contact info
- [ ] HIPAA authorizations (all providers)
- [ ] LOR to 1P carrier
- [ ] LOR to 3P carrier
- [ ] 3P declaration page (policy limits)
- [ ] Police report or incident documentation

**Required before demand:**
- [ ] All medical records from treating providers
- [ ] All medical bills (itemized)
- [ ] Balance confirmations from all providers
- [ ] MRI/imaging reports (if applicable)
- [ ] Property damage photos or repair estimate
- [ ] Lost wage documentation (if claimed)

### Record Red Flags
- Medical records with inconsistent dates
- Bills that don't match treatment dates in records
- Missing pages or incomplete records
- Records from providers not listed in intake
- Charges significantly above usual/customary rates
- Treatment notes that are template/copy-paste without individualization

### Missing Document Priority
1. **Critical (blocks case progress):** 3P dec page, police report, medical records for major providers
2. **Important (needed for demand):** Balance confirmations, imaging reports, lost wage docs
3. **Helpful (strengthens case):** Property damage photos, witness statements, scene photos

## XII. Firm-Specific Preferences

### Fee Structure
- **Pre-litigation contingency:** 25%
- **Post-litigation contingency:** 33-35%
- Costs advanced by firm, reimbursed from settlement

### Lien Reduction Targets
- Target 40-50% reduction on provider liens
- Target 50%+ reduction on health insurance subrogation (non-ERISA)
- ERISA plans: attempt reduction but prepare for lower success rate
- Med-Pay subrogation: request fee waiver first

### Demand Letter Style
- Professional, factual tone — avoid emotional language
- Include specific medical terminology from records
- Organize by: Liability, Injuries/Treatment, Damages, Demand
- Always include 30-day time-limit language
- Policy limits demands: reference bad faith exposure
- Specific amount demands: show multiplier calculation in demand section

### Approval Workflow
- All demand letters are DRAFT until attorney review
- Settlement offers above $25,000 require attorney approval before acceptance
- Lien reductions must be documented in writing
- Client must approve final disbursement breakdown
